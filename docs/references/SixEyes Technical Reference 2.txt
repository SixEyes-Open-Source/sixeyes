============================================================
SixEyes – Technical Reference (PCB Revision)
============================================

---

1. Project Purpose & Scope

---

SixEyes is a low-cost, open-source, research robotic arm platform
designed for Vision–Language–Action (VLA) experimentation.

The system focuses on:

• Collecting high-quality multimodal datasets
• Enabling real-time VLA policy execution
• Using accessible, reusable hardware
• Maintaining deterministic low-level control

The system architecture intentionally separates:

Embedded control (real-time)
from
AI inference (high compute).

Embedded hardware focuses strictly on sensing,
motor control, and safety.

All perception, planning, and machine learning
runs on an external laptop.

---

3. Hardware Architecture

---

---

## 3.1 Leader Arm (Teleoperation Controller)

The leader arm captures human motion input using potentiometers.
These analog signals represent target joint positions.

MCU:
ESP32-S3-WROOM-1

ADC Domain:
ADC1 only is used to avoid WiFi interference issues.

Inputs:
6× potentiometers.

Leader Pinout:

Pot 1  → GPIO1
Pot 2  → GPIO2
Pot 3  → GPIO3
Pot 4  → GPIO4
Pot 5  → GPIO5
Pot 6  → GPIO6

UART Link to Follower:

TX → GPIO17
RX → GPIO18

Power:

Leader board may be powered by:

• 3.3V from follower PCB
or
• Local LDO if used standalone.

---

## 3.2 Follower Arm (Actuated Robot Arm)

The follower arm executes motion using stepper motors
and servo actuators.

Final Degrees of Freedom:

Base            → 1× NEMA17
Shoulder        → 2× NEMA17 (torque split)
Elbow           → 1× NEMA17
Wrist Pitch     → 1× Servo
Wrist Yaw       → 1× Servo
Gripper         → 1× Servo

Total:

• 4 stepper channels
• 3 servo channels
• 6 DOF system

---

## 3.3 Stepper Motor Drivers

Drivers:

4× TMC2209 modules

Configuration:

• PDN_UART mode enabled
• StallGuard used for homing
• VIO = 3.3V
• VM  = 24V

Follower ESP32-S3 Pinout
(TMC2209 ×4)

J1 – Base

STEP → GPIO4
DIR  → GPIO5
EN   → GPIO6
PDN_UART → GPIO7

J2 – Shoulder A

STEP → GPIO8
DIR  → GPIO9
EN   → GPIO10
PDN_UART → GPIO11

J3 – Shoulder B

STEP → GPIO12
DIR  → GPIO13
EN   → GPIO14
PDN_UART → GPIO15

J4 – Elbow

STEP → GPIO16
DIR  → GPIO17
EN   → GPIO18
PDN_UART → GPIO21

Electrical Notes:

• 1k series resistor on each PDN_UART line
• 4.7k pull-up resistor to 3.3V on PDN_UART
• EN pins controlled by safety task
• All grounds must be common

---

## 3.4 Servo Outputs

Servos operate on a dedicated high-current rail.

Servo Channels:

Wrist Pitch → GPIO35
Wrist Yaw   → GPIO36
Gripper     → GPIO37

Servo Power:

• Powered from 6.6V rail
• Separate wide traces required
• Large bulk capacitance near headers

Recommended Bulk Capacitance:

≥ 1000 µF electrolytic near servo rail

---

## 3.5 Leader–Follower Communication

Communication between arms uses a dedicated UART.

Follower Side:

RX (Leader TX) → GPIO38
TX (Leader RX) → GPIO39

Packet Type:

Absolute joint target values.

Benefits:

• Deterministic latency
• Works without laptop
• Allows teleoperation fallback mode

---

## 3.6 Camera Architecture (Revised)

Cameras are NOT connected through the follower PCB.

Instead, cameras connect directly to the laptop.

Architecture:

Camera → USB cable → Laptop

Reasons for this design:

• Eliminates USB hub complexity
• Reduces PCB routing difficulty
• Avoids high-speed differential design constraints
• Improves reliability
• Simplifies assembly

The ESP32 boards handle only:

• motor control
• sensing
• telemetry

Laptop handles:

• camera streaming
• VLA perception
• dataset logging

4. Power Architecture

---

---

## 4.1 Main Power Input

Primary input:

24V DC
Recommended supply: ≥10A

This rail directly powers:

• Stepper motor drivers (VM)
• Buck converters

---

## 4.2 6.6V Rail (Servo Power)

Converter:

XL4016 Buck Converter
Integrated directly onto the PCB.

Output Voltage:

6.6V

Purpose:

• Power MG996R / MG995 class servos

Design Notes:

• XL4016 supports high current capability
• Suitable for multiple high-torque servos
• Large inductor and heatsinking area required

Recommended Layout:

• Wide power traces
• Thermal vias under converter
• Large output capacitor bank

Typical Components:

• 330 µH inductor (module-dependent)
• ≥1000 µF output capacitance
• Schottky diode rated ≥10A

---

## 4.3 3.3V Rail (Logic Power)

Converter:

MP1584 Buck Converter
Integrated directly onto the PCB.

Output Voltage:

3.3V

Loads:

• ESP32-S3 (Follower)
• ESP32-S3 (Leader if powered externally)
• TMC2209 VIO
• Pull-ups and logic circuits

Advantages of MP1584:

• Compact footprint
• High efficiency
• 3A output capability
• Good availability

Recommended Capacitors:

Input: ≥22 µF
Output: ≥22 µF + 0.1 µF decoupling

---

## 4.4 Power Rail Summary

24V Rail

Powers:

• Stepper drivers (VM)
• Buck converters

6.6V Rail (XL4016)

Powers:

• Servos

3.3V Rail (MP1584)

Powers:

• ESP32 logic
• Driver logic
• communication circuitry

---

## 4.5 Grounding Strategy

All grounds share a common star point
near the main power input.

Important routing rules:

• Servo return currents must not pass under MCU
• Buck converter ground loops should be tight
• Logic ground should remain quiet

Recommended Layout Strategy:

High-current zone:

• 24V input
• XL4016
• servo connectors

Logic zone:

• ESP32
• MP1584
• UART lines
• step/dir traces

6. Mechanical Design Implications

---

Shoulder Joint

Two NEMA17 motors are used.

Reasons:

• shoulder experiences highest torque
• improves reliability
• reduces shaft stress

Target Performance:

Payload:
500 g

Reach:
500 mm

Gear Reduction:

Cycloidal gearbox
25:1 ratio

Because the system uses
3D-printed mechanical components
without bearings,
extra torque margin is required.

timestamp

8. Safety Architecture

---

Safety principles:

Motors disabled on boot.

Motion only enabled after heartbeat.

Heartbeat Timeout:

~500 ms

Safety Control:

Driver EN pins controlled
by safety supervisor task.

If laptop communication stops:

• drivers disable
• motion halts

This prevents runaway motion.

---

9. Mechanical Design Constraints

---

• Cycloidal gearboxes are fully 3D-printed
• Software compensates for backlash
• StallGuard used for homing
• Wiring routed internally where possible
• Camera mounting must avoid wrist occlusion

---

10. Cost and Sustainability Goals

---

Design philosophy:

Reuse hardware wherever possible.

Typical reused components:

• NEMA17 motors from old 3D printers
• MG996R servos
• generic stepper drivers

Structural components:

• entirely 3D-printed

Target BOM:

~$250 USD
(excluding prints and laptop)

12. Summary Statement

---

SixEyes is a reproducible,
low-cost research platform for
Vision–Language–Action robotics.

The embedded system focuses on:

deterministic control,
hardware safety,
and real-time actuation.

All intelligence is handled externally
by a laptop running ROS2 and VLA models.

This separation keeps the hardware simple,
robust, and accessible to researchers.
